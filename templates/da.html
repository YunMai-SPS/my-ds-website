How the Financial Ties Between Industry and Providers Affect the Medical Cost?

Since Open Payments Program which was established as part of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) is designed to promote transparency and provide information about financial relationships between healthcare providers (such as physicians and teaching hospitals) and pharmaceutical and medical device industries to the public, many data enthusiasts have been studying Open Payments Data to address the concerns about the potential influence of financial relationships between healthcare providers and industry on the high healthcare costs of the United States.

What are the medical cost in different state?

In 2016, the medical cost was inbalanced across 50 states. Top 4 states expensing highest Medicare medical costs were CA($7.7M), FL($7.5M), NY($5.7M), and TX($5.6M). Top 4 state having highest drug cost were FL($1.6M), CA($1.3M), TX($1.2M), and NY($0.8M). Top 4 state having highest healthcare industry payments were CA($1.6M), TX($1.3M), NY($1.2M), and FL($1.1M).

How industry payments influence Medicare costs?

Hypothesis: The industry payments or more generally medical marketing can impact providers’ attitudes and behavior in the US, such as prescribing brand-name drugs, even when equally effective generic substitutes are available, billing for more medical services or charging a higher cost per service, which would lead to an higher level of total Medicare costs, broken down by medical services and drugs.

There is a statistically significant positive association between industry payments and Medicare costs. According to the model, a 10% difference in industry payments was associated with ~7.62% (95% CI: 7.59%, 7.66%) higher total medical (non-drug) costs per provider. Given that the industry payment median was $255 and the Medicare cost median was $97,793, a change of $25 in industry payment would associated with a change of $1,056 in medical cost.
